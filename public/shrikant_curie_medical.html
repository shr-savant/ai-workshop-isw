The problem

In 2020, a colleague and I started a company called Curie Medical. We'd both spent years in engineering — 3D modeling, simulation, machine learning — and we kept noticing the same thing when we looked at medical imaging: the technology was decades behind.

An MRI scan isn't one image. It's a stack of hundreds of cross-sectional slices. Black and white. Flat. A trained radiologist can read them. But a surgeon planning to remove a tumor surrounded by blood vessels, nerves, and bone? They study each slice, one by one, mentally assembling a 3D picture that only exists in their head. pause Every time. For every patient. Every surgery.

The data was there. The visualization wasn't. And we knew how to build it.

Alice

We built software we called Alice. An MRI scan is, at its core, intensity values organized in 3D space — and we knew how to turn 3D data into 3D models. Alice reads the scan and generates a rotatable, zoomable anatomical model in minutes. The name came from the idea that giving a surgeon that view should feel like what Alice felt stepping into Wonderland — suddenly seeing something familiar from a completely different angle.

The website is curiemedical.com. beat Give a surgeon a 3D model of a patient's anatomy before they enter the operating room, and you've changed the surgery before it's started.

Where AI came in

But there was a second problem — one that had existed long before Alice, and that Alice, on its own, hadn't solved.

In conventional imaging, surgeons don't just read scans. They also do what's called segmentation — manually tracing which regions of the scan belong to which organ. This cluster is bone. This one is the tumor. This is a vessel. Done by hand, on a stack of 2D slices, it can take hours on a complex case. And it has to be done fresh for every patient.

Alice gave them a 3D model. But that model was still one undifferentiated mass of anatomy — everything visible, nothing labeled. Segmentation still had to happen the same way it always had. pause We'd solved the visualization. We hadn't solved the segmentation.

Here's where we made a bet. In an MRI scan, signal intensity isn't random. It varies predictably by tissue type — bone reads differently from muscle, muscle reads differently from a vessel, a vessel reads differently from a tumor. That signal encodes exactly what's there. So we built an organ segmentation algorithm that reads those intensity patterns and labels each region automatically. beat Now the surgeon doesn't just get a 3D model. They get a 3D model where every organ is a separate, isolated group. Show only the vascular system. Hide the bone. Isolate the tumor.

What used to take hours now happens in the background while the coffee brews.

The takeaway

Curie Medical is a bootstrapped startup. Two people. No VC round. We didn't have an enterprise budget — we had domain expertise and a specific problem we understood well enough to know what to ask of the data.

AI didn't require us to be a big company. It required us to understand the problem. pause The intensity signal was sitting in every MRI scan in every hospital in the world. The algorithm just hadn't been built yet. We built it. And what it gives surgeons is time back, cognitive load reduced, and in surgery — cognitive clarity is not a comfort. It's a safety margin.

That's what AI looks like when you apply it to a specific, well-understood problem with real stakes. Not magic. Pattern recognition, put to work where it matters.
