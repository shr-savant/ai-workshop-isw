Act I — The origin

I want to take you back about thirty years. No internet. No cloud. No GPU clusters. Just a fresh graduate, newly employed, working nights and weekends at home on an idea that felt slightly insane at the time.

I was writing software to simulate vehicle systems — using a neural network. A neural network that could be trained to simulate any part of the vehicle: the suspension system, the steering system, the engine mounts, and so on. pause Think about that for a moment. Neural networks in the mid-nineties. The data you needed? You had to manufacture it yourself. The compute you needed? You had to beg for it. And the community of people who could even talk to you about this? You could fit them in a small room.

Nothing dramatic came out of that project. No breakthrough, no publication that shook the world. The tools just weren't there yet. beat But something stuck with me. The idea that you could teach a machine to reason about a physical system — not just calculate, but learn — that never left.

Act II — The battle inside the building

Fast forward to SolidWorks. I've been there over a decade. And I'm watching how engineers work — really watching — and something is bothering me. The state of the art for guiding a designer through a workflow was a checklist. Step one, check. Step two, check. Step three, check. pause We were building some of the most sophisticated 3D design software in the world, and our best idea for intelligence was... a to-do list.

I knew there was a better way. And by now, machine learning had caught up to the idea I'd been carrying for twenty years. So I started talking about it internally. And the question I heard — every single time, from almost everyone — was the same: "We're a computer-aided design company. Why do we need machine learning?"

And I'd explain: because ML can learn what good design looks like. It can guide a designer the way a senior engineer would — not with a checklist, but with judgment. It can catch mistakes before they become expensive. It can compress months of iteration into hours. pause And then they'd say: "Okay. Show me."

Act III — Two years, one moment

So I did. I went around the company and I asked people: what problem would you most want machine learning to solve? I listened. I gathered a team. And for two years, we built.

Then came the moment. Our annual company gathering — over five thousand people in the room. We put the prototype on the main stage. beat And it worked.

The reaction wasn't polite applause. It was electric. And here's what made it unforgettable: the people who came up afterward were the very same ones who had looked me in the eye and asked "why do we even need this?" Now they were pitching me — each one with a design problem they wanted machine learning to solve. Problems they'd been sitting on for years, assuming nobody could touch them.

But what struck me most wasn't the problems they already knew about — it was the ones they'd never thought to ask before. The demo opened a door to a whole different floor. Showing something working doesn't just solve — it unlocks. pause

Now, the excitement was real — but the road from prototype to product was anything but smooth. No data to train on. No compute at scale. And because we built for the cloud version of the product, we had no infrastructure to collect or store the data we needed — we had to build that from scratch too. Three hard problems at once. beat But we had something we didn't have before: believers.

The conversation flipped entirely. From "why do we need this?" to "how do we get more — and fast?" pause Today, every new project at that company is an AI project. People can't get enough.

The handoff

Thirty years. From a neural network nobody could run, to five thousand people who wouldn't stop talking. I'm not telling you this to impress you — I'm telling you because you are standing at the exact same inflection point I was at before any of that happened. The skepticism, the uncertainty, the feeling that maybe this isn't for me — I had all of that. beat And I can tell you what's on the other side.

Enough talking from us. Let's actually do this. Rik, show them.
